
# Determiners (Bepalers / Lidwoorden)

Determiners introduce a noun and specify which or how much.

### Articles (Lidwoorden)

One of the hardest aspects of Dutch is knowing whether a noun takes **de** or **het**.

Some rules of thumb:

- All plural nouns use **de**
- Diminutives always use **het** (*het huisje, het meisje*)
- Words ending in **-ing, -heid, -schap, -ie** are usually **de**
- Most nouns referring to people follow natural gender (de man, de vrouw)

| | Definite | Indefinite |
|-|----------|------------|
| de-word | **de** | **een** |
| het-word | **het** | **een** |

- *de tafel* — the table; *een tafel* — a table
- *het boek* — the book; *een boek* — a book

> When in doubt, look it up — native speakers sometimes disagree too!

### Quantifying determiners

| Dutch | English |
|-------|---------|
| **elk / elke** | each, every |
| **ieder / iedere** | each, every |
| **alle** | all |
| **sommige** | some |
| **veel** | many / much |
| **weinig** | few / little |
| **meer** | more |
| **meest** | most |
| **genoeg** | enough |
| **geen** | no / not any |

> *elk / ieder* take **-e** before de-words: *elke dag* (every day), *elk kind*
> (every child — het-word, no -e in older style, though *elk(e)* is now often used for both).
