# Pronouns in Dutch  *(A1)*

Dutch has several categories of pronouns.
Each category has a specific role in a sentence.

And many have two forms: a **full (stressed)** form and a **reduced (unstressed)** form.

## Personal Subject pronouns

| Person | Full form | Reduced form | English |
|--------|-----------|--------------|---------|
| 1st sg | **ik** | 'k | I |
| 1st pl | **wij** | we | we |
| 2nd sg | **jij** | je | you (singular) |
| 2nd sg formal | **u** | u | you (formal) |
| 2nd pl | **jullie** | — | you (plural) |
| 3rd sg masc | **hij** | ie | he |
| 3rd sg fem | **zij** | ze | she |
| 3rd sg neuter | **het** | 't | it |
| 3rd pl | **zij** | ze | they |

> The reduced forms (je, ze, we) are used in everyday speech. The full forms (jij, zij, wij) are used for emphasis or contrast: *Jij doet het, niet ik.* ("You do it, not me.")

## Personal Object pronouns

| Person | Form | English |
|--------|------|---------|
| 1st sg | **mij / me** | me |
| 1st pl | **ons** | us |
| 2nd sg | **jou / je** | you |
| 2nd formal | **u** | you |
| 2nd pl | **jullie** | you |
| 3rd sg masc | **hem** | him |
| 3rd sg fem | **haar** | her |
| 3rd sg neuter | **het / 't** | it |
| 3rd pl | **hen / hun / ze** | them |

3rd pl - *hen* vs *hun* vs *ze*:

- *hen* is used after prepositions (*voor hen* — for them);
- *hun* is used as indirect object (*ik geef hun een boek* — I give them a book).
- In practice, *ze* is used most often in speech.

## Reflexive Pronouns (Wederkerige / Reflexieve voornaamwoorden)

Used when the subject and object refer to the same person.

Dutch uses **zich** for third person and formal second person; other persons use the object pronoun.

| Person | Reflexive | English |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| 1st sg | **me / mezelf** | myself |
| 1st pl | **ons / onszelf** | ourselves |
| 2nd sg | **je / jezelf** | yourself |
| 2nd formal | **zich / uzelf** | yourself |
| 2nd pl | **je / jezelf** | yourselves |
| 3rd sg/pl | **zich / zichzelf** | himself / herself / itself / themselves |

> The **-zelf** form adds emphasis: *Hij waste zich.* (He washed himself.) vs. *Hij waste zichzelf.* (He washed himself — emphatic.)

Common reflexive verbs:

- *zich wassen* — to wash oneself
- *zich voelen* — to feel (oneself)
- *zich herinneren* — to remember
- *zich vergissen* — to make a mistake
- *zich voorstellen* — to introduce oneself / to imagine

## Demonstrative / Referential Pronouns (Verwijswoorden)

Used to point to or refer back to specific instance of something.

Dutch distinguishes **near** vs. **far** and **de-words** vs. **het-words**.

| | de-word | he**t**-word | English |
|------|---------|----------|---------|
| Near | **deze** | di**t** | this |
| Far | **die** | da**t** | that |

Examples:

- *deze man* — this man (de man, near)
- *dit kind* — this child (het kind, near)
- *die vrouw* — that woman (de vrouw, far)
- *dat huis* — that house (het huis, far)

### As standalone references

*die* and *dat* are also used to refer back to a previously mentioned noun:

- *Ik zag een film. Die was geweldig.* (I saw a film. That one was great.)
- *Hij kocht een huis. Dat staat in Amsterdam.* (He bought a house. It's in Amsterdam.)

## The Possessive Pronouns

Possessives determine **ownership**.

Dutch has both

- **possessive pronouns** that sit in front of a noun,
- and **standalone** forms used without a following noun.

| Person | Possessive | English |
|--------|----------------|---------|
| 1st sg | **mijn**  | my |
| 1st pl | **onze** de / **ons** het | our |
| 2nd sg (informal) | **jouw / je**  | your |
| 2nd (formal) | **uw** | your |
| 2nd pl | **jullie**  | your |
| 3rd sg masc | **zijn**  | his |
| 3rd sg fem | **haar**  | her |
| 3rd sg neuter | **zijn**  | its |
| 3rd pl | **hun**  | their |

### **onze** vs **ons**

Each pronoun has the **same form before any noun**, with **one exception**: *ons* / *onze*.

Use **onze** before de-words and **ons** before het-words.

| de-word | het-word |
|---------|----------|
| **onze** auto | **ons** huis |
| **onze** moeder | **ons** kind |
| **onze** stad | **ons** land |
| **onze** vrienden (always plural → de) | — |

> Plurals are always de-words, so they always take **onze**: *onze kinderen*, *onze huizen*.

### Full vs. Reduced Forms

For 2nd person singular, **jouw** is the full (stressed) form; **je** is the reduced (unstressed) form. Use **jouw** for emphasis or contrast.

| Sentence | Tone |
|----------|------|
| *Is dit **je** boek?* | neutral |
| *Is dit **jouw** boek?* | emphatic / contrast (… or mine?) |

The same emphasis principle applies to subject pronouns: *je* vs *jij*, *ze* vs *zij*.

### Standalone Possessives ("mine, yours, his …")

When the possessive *is* the noun (no following noun), Dutch uses one of two structures:

#### Pattern A: **van** + object pronoun

Most common in speech.

| English | Dutch |
|---------|-------|
| mine | **van mij** |
| ours | **van ons** |
| yours (sg) | **van jou** |
| yours (formal) | **van u** |
| yours (pl) | **van jullie** |
| his / its | **van hem** |
| hers | **van haar** |
| theirs | **van hen / van hun** |

Examples:

- *Dit boek is **van mij**.* — This book is mine.
- *Die auto is **van haar**.* — That car is hers.
- *Is deze jas **van jou**?* — Is this coat yours?

#### Pattern B: **de/het** + possessive + **-e**

More formal/written.

| Pronoun | de-form | het-form |
|---------|---------|----------|
| 1st sg | **de mijne** | **het mijne** |
| 2nd sg | **de jouwe** | **het jouwe** |
| 2nd formal | **de uwe** | **het uwe** |
| 3rd sg masc | **de zijne** | **het zijne** |
| 3rd sg fem | **de hare** | **het hare** |
| 1st pl | **de onze** | **het onze** |
| 3rd pl | **de hunne** | **het hunne** |

- *Dit is mijn boek. **Het jouwe** ligt daar.* — This is my book. Yours is over there.
- *Mijn auto is rood. **De hare** is blauw.* — My car is red. Hers is blue.

### Possession Without a Pronoun: **van** + Noun

To express *X's Y*, use **Y van X**.

- *de auto **van** mijn vader* — my father's car
- *het huis **van** Anna* — Anna's house
- *de fiets **van** mijn buurman* — my neighbour's bike

> The English Saxon genitive (*Anna's*) is also seen in informal Dutch: *Anna's huis*. *van + name* is more common.

### Body Parts and Clothing

Dutch tends to use **definite articles** (de/het) instead of possessives when the owner is obvious from context, especially with body parts and clothing.

| English | Dutch |
|---------|-------|
| She broke her arm. | Ze brak **haar / de** arm. (both fine; *de* is common in news) |
| Put your coat on. | Doe **je** jas aan. (possessive used for clarity) |

### Common mistakes

- Using *jullie* with -s, like English: ❌ *jullies* → ✅ *jullie* (uninflected).
- Forgetting *ons* vs *onze*: ❌ *onze huis* → ✅ *ons huis*.
- Using *zijn* for a female owner: ❌ *Anna en **zijn** moeder* → ✅ *Anna en **haar** moeder*.
- Adding -e to standalone possessives without an article: ❌ *mijne* → ✅ *de mijne / het mijne*.
- Translating "of mine" literally: ❌ *een vriend van mijn* → ✅ *een vriend van mij*.
